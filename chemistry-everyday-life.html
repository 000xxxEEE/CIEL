Drug-Target Interaction

How drugs work at the molecular level

Section 16.2
Drug: Low molecular mass chemical (~100–500 u) that interacts with macromolecular targets to produce a biological response. When therapeutic → called a medicine. Use of chemicals for therapeutic effect = chemotherapy.
Drug Targets

🧬 Enzymes

  • Biological catalysts (proteins)
  • Hold substrate at active site via ionic bonds, H-bonds, van der Waals, dipole-dipole
  • Provide functional groups to react with substrate

📡 Receptors

  • Proteins embedded in cell membrane
  • Active site projects outside the cell
  • Receive chemical messengers (neurotransmitters)
  • Change shape on binding → transfer message into cell

🔬 Other Targets

  • Carrier proteins (transport polar molecules across membrane)
  • Nucleic acids (genetic information)
  • Lipids & carbohydrates (structural parts of cell membrane)
Enzyme Inhibition

⚔️ Competitive Inhibition

Drug competes with the natural substrate for the active site. The drug and substrate have similar shapes. Drug blocks the substrate from binding.

🔒 Non-Competitive Inhibition

Drug binds to a different site — the allosteric site. This changes the shape of the active site so the substrate can no longer fit.

⛓️ Irreversible Inhibition

Drug forms a strong covalent bond with the enzyme → enzyme permanently blocked. Body degrades the complex and synthesises new enzyme.

Receptor Interaction

🚫 Antagonists

  • Bind to receptor site
  • Inhibit natural function
  • Block the message
  • Useful when blocking is needed (e.g., antihistamines)

✅ Agonists

  • Mimic the natural messenger
  • Switch ON the receptor
  • Stimulate natural response
  • Useful when messenger is lacking

Antacids

Treating hyperacidity and gastric ulcers

Section 16.3.1
Problem: Overproduction of acid in stomach → irritation, pain, ulcers. Histamine stimulates secretion of pepsin and HCl in the stomach wall.
Traditional Antacids (Control Symptoms Only)

NaHCO₃ — Sodium Hydrogencarbonate

Neutralises acid but excessive use makes stomach alkaline, triggering more acid production. ❌ Not ideal.

Al(OH)₃ / Mg(OH)₂ — Metal Hydroxides

Better — insoluble, so pH doesn't rise above neutrality. Still only treat symptoms, not the cause.

Modern Antacids (Treat the Cause)
Mechanism: Block histamine receptors in stomach wall → less histamine binding → reduced acid secretion. These are receptor antagonists.
Cimetidine
Trade: Tegamet
First breakthrough drug. Prevents histamine from interacting with stomach wall receptors. Was the world's largest-selling drug for a time.
Ranitidine
Trade: Zantac
Discovered after cimetidine; became even more successful.
Why don't antacids affect allergy? Antiallergic and antacid drugs work on different receptors — histamine has multiple receptor subtypes in different locations.

Antihistamines

Blocking histamine's allergic effects

Section 16.3.2
Histamine's roles: Potent vasodilator · contracts smooth muscles of bronchi and gut · relaxes walls of fine blood vessels · causes nasal congestion in cold · triggers allergic response to pollen.
How Antihistamines Work

Mechanism — Competitive Antagonism

Antihistamines compete with histamine for the same receptor binding sites where histamine exerts its allergic effect. They do not destroy histamine — they simply block the receptor, preventing histamine from docking.

Key Antihistamine Drugs
Brompheniramine
Dimetapp / Dimetane
Synthetic antihistamine used for allergies and cold symptoms.
Terfenadine
Seldane
Synthetic antihistamine; acts on histamine receptors in allergic response.
Key distinction: Antiallergic antihistamines work on H₁ receptors (allergy/nasal), while antacid drugs (like cimetidine) block H₂ receptors (stomach acid). This is why they don't interfere with each other.

Tranquilizers

Neurologically active drugs for stress and mental health

Section 16.3.3(a)
Tranquilizers are chemical compounds used to treat stress and mental diseases. They relieve anxiety, stress, irritability, and excitement by inducing a sense of well-being. Form an essential component of sleeping pills. They affect the message transfer mechanism from nerve to receptor.
Mechanism — Depression & Noradrenaline

🔑 Role of Noradrenaline

Noradrenaline is a neurotransmitter that plays a role in mood changes. Low noradrenaline → low signal-sending activity → depression.

💊 Antidepressant Mechanism

Antidepressants inhibit enzymes that catalyse the degradation of noradrenaline. The neurotransmitter is slowly metabolised → activates its receptor for longer → counteracts depression.

Analgesics

Pain-relieving drugs

Section 16.3.3(b)
Analgesics reduce or abolish pain without causing impairment of consciousness, mental confusion, incoordination, or paralysis.
Type 1 — Non-Narcotic (Non-Addictive) Analgesics
Aspirin
Most familiar example
Mechanism: Inhibits synthesis of prostaglandins (which stimulate inflammation and pain).
Uses: Skeletal pain (arthritis), fever reduction (antipyretic), prevents platelet coagulation → used in heart attack prevention.
Paracetamol
Non-narcotic analgesic
Widely used pain and fever reliever. Non-addictive.
Type 2 — Narcotic (Addictive) Analgesics
Narcotic analgesics (opiates) are obtained from the opium poppy. In medicinal doses: relieve pain, produce sleep. In poisonous doses: stupor, coma, convulsions, death.
Morphine
Parent compound. Used for postoperative pain, cardiac pain, terminal cancer pain, childbirth.
Heroin
Diacetyl morphine — homologue of morphine. Highly addictive.
Codeine
Methylmorphine. Used in cough syrups and mild pain relief.
Uses of narcotic analgesics: Relief of postoperative pain · cardiac pain · pains of terminal cancer · childbirth.

Antimicrobials

Antibiotics, antiseptics, and disinfectants

Section 16.3.4
Antimicrobial: Tends to destroy/prevent development or inhibit pathogenic action of microbes — bacteria, fungi, viruses, parasites — selectively.
A. Antibiotics

Definition (Modern)

A substance produced wholly or partly by chemical synthesis that, in low concentrations, inhibits the growth of or destroys microorganisms by intervening in their metabolic processes. Low toxicity to humans.

B. Antiseptics vs Disinfectants

🩹 Antiseptics

  • Applied to living tissues (wounds, cuts, ulcers)
  • Not ingested like antibiotics
  • Dettol = chloroxylenol + terpineol
  • Bithionol — added to soaps
  • Iodine (tincture) — 2–3% in alcohol-water
  • Iodoform — for wounds
  • Boric acid (dilute) — for eyes
  • Furacine, Soframicine
  • 0.2% phenol solution

🏠 Disinfectants

  • Applied to inanimate objects (floors, drains, instruments)
  • Chlorine: 0.2–0.4 ppm in water
  • Sulphur dioxide: very low concentrations
  • 1% phenol solution
Dual action: Same substance can act as antiseptic or disinfectant by varying concentration. E.g., 0.2% phenol = antiseptic, 1% phenol = disinfectant.

Antifertility Drugs

Birth control and family planning

Section 16.3.5
Birth control pills contain a mixture of synthetic estrogen and progesterone derivatives (both hormones). Progesterone suppresses ovulation. Synthetic progesterone derivatives are more potent than natural progesterone.
Key Antifertility Drugs
Norethindrone
Progesterone derivative
Most widely used synthetic progesterone derivative as antifertility drug. Suppresses ovulation.
Ethynylestradiol (Novestrol)
Estrogen derivative
Used in combination with progesterone derivative (norethindrone) in birth control pills.
Context: Antibiotic revolution nearly doubled life expectancy → population explosion → social problems → need for family planning → antifertility drugs.

Soaps & Detergents

Cleansing agents and their chemistry

Section 16.5
Soaps
Soaps = sodium or potassium salts of long chain fatty acids (stearic, oleic, palmitic acids). Made by saponification — heating fat (glyceryl ester of fatty acid) with NaOH or KOH solution.

Saponification Reaction

(C₁₇H₃₅COO)₃C₃H₅ + 3NaOH → 3C₁₇H₃₅COONa + C₃H₅(OH)₃
Fat (glyceryl stearate) + Sodium hydroxide → Sodium stearate (soap) + Glycerol

🔴 Hydrophobic Tail
Long hydrocarbon chain
Repels water, attracts grease/oil
🔵 Hydrophilic Head
–COO⁻Na⁺ ionic group
Attracts water
Cleansing Action of Soap
Soap in water
→
Hydrophobic tails surround oil/grease
→
Hydrophilic heads face water
→
Micelle forms
→
Grease washes away
Why Soaps Don't Work in Hard Water

Hard Water Problem

Hard water contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions. These react with soap to form insoluble calcium and magnesium soaps (scum) — useless for cleaning. The gummy precipitate adheres to cloth fibres and prevents even dye absorption.

Types of Soaps

Toilet Soaps

Better grades of fats/oils, excess alkali removed, colour and perfumes added.

Floating Soaps

Air bubbles beaten in before hardening.

Transparent Soaps

Dissolved in ethanol, excess solvent evaporated.

Medicated Soaps

Medicinal substances added.

Shaving Soaps

Contain glycerol (prevents drying) + rosin (forms sodium rosinate, lathers well).

Laundry Soaps

Contain fillers: sodium rosinate, sodium silicate, borax, sodium carbonate.

Synthetic Detergents
Synthetic detergents have all properties of soaps but contain no soap. They work in both soft and hard water — do not form insoluble precipitates with Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ ions.
TypeDescriptionExampleUse
Anionic Sodium salts of sulphonated long chain alcohols or hydrocarbons. Anionic part does cleansing. Sodium dodecylbenzenesulphonate Household work, toothpastes
Cationic Quaternary ammonium salts of amines. Long hydrocarbon chain + positive N. Germicidal properties. Expensive. Cetyltrimethylammonium bromide Hair conditioners, germicides
Non-ionic No ions in constitution. Remove grease by micelle formation like soaps. Stearic acid + polyethyleneglycol product Liquid dishwashing detergents
⚠ Environmental Problem: Highly branched hydrocarbon chains in detergents cannot be degraded by bacteria → accumulate in rivers/ponds → foaming and water pollution. Solution: Use unbranched (straight chain) detergents — biodegradable.
